About Me

Hi, I'm Jaweria Qaiser (Ja-where-ya Kai-zer)—a social psychology researcher and science communicator. I'm currently a PhD candidate at the University of Toronto (Canada), where I study prosocial emotions (like empathy and compassion), interpersonal relationships (strangers, friends, couples), and morality both emotionally and physiologically. My research uses psychophysiological methods to ask foundational questions about human connection.

  • Is compassion a finite resource?
    • While dominant narratives suggest that repeated exposure to suffering leads to emotional exhaustion and declining compassion, my research challenges this view. Compassion may remain stable or even increase over time, while what actually declines is our emotional bandwidth and perceived ability to cope—leading to what we call “compassion fatigue.” This distinction invites a reconceptualization of how we measure and support compassionate engagement.
  • How do our bodies respond when we feel compassion or empathy across different affective contexts?
    • I explore whether physiological synchrony (Qaiser et al., 2023)—such as changes in heart rate or skin conductance—emerges during empathic exchanges, and how these responses vary depending on the emotional tone of the interaction (e.g., suffering vs. joy). These investigations help illuminate the embodied nature of empathy and the subtle ways our bodies attune to others, even when verbal communication is absent or limited.
  • How do we respond to others when we hold opposing moral values to them?
    • This question probes the intersection of empathy and moral conflict. I’m interested in whether people can still engage empathically with those whose values they reject, and what psychological mechanisms either facilitate or inhibit that process. This line of inquiry has implications for polarization, dialogue, and the emotional labor of bridging ideological divides.
